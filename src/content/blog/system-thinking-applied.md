Beyond engineering, I have always gravitated toward creative disciplines. Art has long been a parallel interest in my life — a very different environment, yet one that has shaped how I think about structure, growth and intention. Art can be highly abstract — and so can software. Creativity becomes particularly important in situations without established patterns or prior solutions. At times, ideas begin to align, and the structure starts to emerge.

When working on greenfield systems, two complementary mindsets would often emerge.

One sees the system as something that grows — an evolving structure that gradually gains coherence, internal logic and character. It needs care, consistency and long-term thinking.

The other approaches it as a sequence of constrained problems — almost a duel with complexity. Each obstacle must be understood, reduced and resolved. Precision matters. One wrong assumption can cascade.

I recognize myself in both perspectives.

This duality is not accidental. Architecture — in the physical sense — was a recurring theme in my family environment. Large structures demand correct foundations, thoughtful load distribution and patience. Scale does not forgive improvisation. The same applies to distributed systems.

Earlier in life, I was drawn to mechanical engineering and invention. Building physical machines is unforgiving: material limits, manufacturing constraints, irreversibility. Discovering software revealed a different kind of leverage. With code, complex mechanisms can be designed, reshaped and evolved with comparatively light tools — yet the structural responsibility remains just as real.

There is also the layer of pure logic. I played chess seriously for many years. A long game requires sustained concentration, positional thinking and respect for compounding consequences. Large-scale systems feel similar. You move from one constraint to another, building a position that must hold under pressure.

It is the combination of these influences — creativity, structural awareness, engineering discipline and strategic thinking — that makes me comfortable in large, complex projects.

I do not see software as just implementation. I see it as something that is designed, constructed and gradually brought into structural integrity. Without a certain degree of internal drive and emotional investment, truly excellent systems are rarely created.
